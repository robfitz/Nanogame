A Brief History of Nano

  1. 2012

    In 2012 the number of patents for new nanotechnology inventions was XXXXx as high as the year before, with over XXXX new ideas and technologies submitted. The ability we have to manipulate the nanoscale is going to shape our shared future. The question now is; how?

  2. 2007

    A researcher called Angela Belcher and her colleagues at MIT built a nano-scale lithium-ion battery using a virus (nonharmful to humans), they have the same energy properties as cutting-edge rechargeable batteries and making them doesn’t harm the environment. Viruses could soon be powering your electronic devices.

  3. 2004

    This year Britain’s Royal Society and the Royal Academy of Engineering published Nanoscience and Nanotechnologies: Opportunities and Uncertainties. This document was a call to arms – the age of nanotechnology is upon us, as we progress we must address the social, environmental, health and ethical issues that this new world opens up, along with its promised opportunities.

  4. 1999 - 2000's

    In this period nanotech properly enters the realm of consumer products – from tougher car bumpers to antibacterial socks, tennis rackets that hit balls back faster to cosmetics that penetrate the skin. Products developed in clean-rooms and laboratories start to change the everyday lives of ordinary folk.

  5. 1986

    The birth of the Atomic Force Microscope (AMF). A machine that, unlike the STM which is limited to conductor or semiconductor surfaces, allows us to make images of almost any surface, including biological materials.

  6. 1981

    Scanning/Tunneling Microscope

    Invented by Gerd Binning and Heinrich Rohrer at IBM’s research lab in Zurich, the Scanning Tunnelling Microscope (STM) scans a tip of a probe above the surface of something, sensing tiny differences in how far electrons travel, and so mapping the surface.

    But the STM isn’t just a tool for not only investigating and imaging individual atoms and their structures, the probe can manipulating them too! Humans could now move around the fundamental units of the universe.

  7. 1974

    The term nanotechnology was used for the first time by Norio Taniguchi of the Tokyo Science University.

  8. 1951

    Erwin Muller created a new kind of microscope, the Field Ion Microscope (FIM). This is so powerful that for the first time in our history, the arrangement of individual atoms, the particles whose existence we’ve been debating for thousands of years, can finally be seen.

  9. 1931

    It shouldn’t be surprising that the history of nanoscience is tied into the history of being able to look at very small things. Light microscopes could only look at objects larger than the wavelength of visible light (0.4 – 0.7 micrometers). But Max Knot and Erst Ruska used an accelerated beam of electrons in the Transmitting Electron Microscope (TEM), with their much shorter wavelengths the electrons could help image objects previously far too small to see, the door was flung open to an invisible world.

  10. 1904 - 1913

    With no way of directly observing our universal building blocks, there were a number of exciting new theories about how atoms were structured. J. J. Thomson (1904) proposed that atoms were modelled with positive and negative charges mixed up inside them like a ‘plum pudding. Rutherford’s 1911 research proved this model to be incorrect, that there was a central nucleus.

    And in 1913 Niels Bohr described the atom as having electrons that travel in circular orbits around a small, positively charged nucleus. This was closer, but a century later we are still expanding our understanding of atomic structures and properties.

  11. 1827

    To represent the world until now, human hands had to draw, paint or engrave. In 1827 Joseph Niepce produced the first true photograph, a representation of the world formed by radiation. He used a film containing silver halides, when exposed to light these decomposed into nanoparticles of silver, these formed the pixels of the image, the first photograph.

  12. 1674

    Telescopes had recently given humans their first glimpses of huge objects far away, and using the same revolutionary ground glass technology, Anton van Leeuwenhoek, a fabric trader, used to scrutinizing his weave, built the first microscope.

    With just one lens he suddenly found himself exploring a world much closer, where pond water swarmed with previously invisible animalcules, and fleas were complex and hairy. As the technology spread were able to discover the hidden worlds all around, and others more inside us.

  13. 400 AD

    Perhaps the first use of nanotechnology which survives to this day is the ancient Roman Lycurgus Cup. A glassware flask that changes from green when illuminated from the outside, to red, when illuminated from within. It does this because the glassmakers integrated nanoparticles of gold and silver into the vessel, when these metals are broken into tiny particles they have unusual optical effects, which the Romans exploited.

  14. 500 BC

    In Ancient Greece, a philosopher called Leucippusand his pupil Democritus proposed something literally groundbreaking; that all matter is composed of small indivisible particles called atoms, of which everything is composed and of which there are different kinds.

    If you cut an object down in size, again and again you would eventually arrive at these indestructible structures. It would take two and a half thousand years to prove how close they were.

  15. 1.8 million years ago...

    No-one knows how long ago our ancestor, homo erectus, first emerged. But it could have been almost 2 million years ago, and it could also have coincided with a remarkable thing which only humans do – create and use fire to change the properties of the world around us.

    With fire we could cook our food and keep ourselves warm. We could see things that before were hidden. We might look a little different from our ancestors, but our compulsion to play, investigate and manipulate the world around us is very likely unchanged.
