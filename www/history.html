A Brief History of Nano

  1. 2012

    Nanotechnology now impacts us all, whether we know it or not. In an explosion of research papers and patents, nanoscience is shaping our lives from communications systems to cancer treatment, space transport to high fashion. In the words of Professor Gabriel Aeppli of the London Centre for Nanotechnology: “I wouldn't call it a discipline, I'd call it a toolkit”.

  2. 2007

    A researcher called Angela Belcher and her colleagues at MIT build a nano-scale lithium-ion battery harnessing viruses (non-harmful to humans). They have the same energy capacity and power as cutting-edge rechargeable batteries, but their manufacture has less of a detrimental impact on the environment. Viruses may soon be powering your electronic devices.

  3. 2004

    Britain’s Royal Society and the Royal Academy of Engineering publish “Nanoscience and Nanotechnologies: Opportunities and Uncertainties”. This document is a call to arms – the age of nanotechnology is upon us: as we progress we must address the social, environmental, health and ethical issues that this new world opens up, along with its promised opportunities.

  4. 1999 - 2000's

    Nanotechnology enters the realm of consumer products – from tougher car bumpers to antibacterial socks, more powerful tennis rackets that hit longer-lasting tennis balls back to cosmetics that penetrate the skin. Products developed in clean-rooms and laboratories slowly begin to change the everyday lives of ordinary folk.

  5. 1985

    Binnig, Quate and Gerber invent the Atomic Force Microscope (AFM), a new member of the scanning probe microcopy family. For the first time, using forces on the nanoscale, three dimensional topographical imaging can be carried out on non-conductive and conductive surfaces. This allows routine imaging of molecular and biological systems without the need for destructive sample preparations.

  6. 1981

    Scanning/Tunneling Microscope

    Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer, at IBM’s research lab in Zurich, invent the Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM) and are subsequently awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics (1986). By placing a sharp metallic tip just above a conductive surface, electrons are allowed to “tunnel” between the two metals.

    By sensing the electron flow between tip and sample as the probe moves across the surface a three dimensional “map” of the sample can be obtained. But the STM isn’t limited to investigating and imaging individual surface atoms and crystal structures, the probe can manipulate them too! Humans can now move around the fundamental building blocks of the universe.

  7. 1974

    The term nanotechnology is used for the first time by Norio Taniguchi of the Tokyo Science University.

  8. 1953

    “This structure has novel features which are of considerable biological interest” wrote Cambridge researchers James Crick and Francis Watson in 1953, as they published their double-helix model of the structure of DNA. Inspired by X-ray photographs from Rosalind Franklin, the nano-machinery that codes and creates us is perhaps now the most famous image in science.

  9. 1951

    Erwin Muller creates a new kind of microscope, the Field Ion Microscope (FIM). This is so powerful that for the first time in our history the arrangement of individual atoms, the particles whose existence we’ve been debating for thousands of years, can finally be seen.

  10. 1931

    Max Knoll and Ernst Ruska use an accelerated beam of electrons to image materials, building the first Transmitting Electron Microscope (TEM). Prior to this, microscopes using light sources can only image objects larger than the wavelength of visible light (a theoretical resolution limit of around 200 nm). With their much shorter wavelengths, the electrons image objects previously far too small to see: the door flies open to an invisible world.

  11. 1904 - 1913

    With no way of directly observing our universal building blocks, a number of exciting new theories are proposed about how atoms are constructed. J. J. Thomson (1904) proposes that atoms are modeled with positive and negative charges mixed up inside them like a ‘plum pudding. This is ultimately shown to be false by Rutherford’s gold foil experiment. His interpretation of the experimental data leads him to formulate the Rutherford model of the atom in 1911 — that a very small positively charged nucleus, containing much of the atom's mass, is orbited by low-mass electrons.

    This is further expanded in 1913 by Niels Bohr who introduces the concept of electrons traveling in orbits around a small, positively charged nucleus. Bohr also introduces the idea that an electron can drop from a higher-energy orbit to a lower one, in the process emitting a photon (light quantum) of discrete energy. This becomes a basis for quantum theory. A century later we are still expanding our understanding of atomic structures and properties.

  12. 1827

    To represent the world until now, human hands had to draw, paint or engrave. In 1827 Joseph Niépce produces the first true photograph, a representation of the world formed by radiation. He uses a film containing silver halides which, when exposed to light, decompose into nanoparticles of silver. These form the pixels of the image, the first photograph.

  13. 1674

    Telescopes have recently given humans their first glimpses of huge objects far away. Using the same revolutionary ground glass technology, Anton van Leeuwenhoek, a fabric trader, builds the first microscope to scrutinize his weave.

    With just one lens he suddenly finds himself exploring a hidden world, where pond water swarms with previously invisible animalcules, and fleas are complex and hairy. As the technology spreads, the invisible worlds all around us and within us are scrutinized and explored.

  14. 400 AD

    Roman glassblowers unwittingly use the principles of nanotechnology to make an extraordinarily ingenious “Lycurgus Cup”. This is a glassware flask that changes from green when illuminated with diffuse light, to red when illuminated with direct light. It does this because the glassmakers include quantities of gold and silver salts into the glass. When these turn into metallic nanoparticles they exhibit unusual optical effects, which the Romans exploit. This is one of the earliest objects to survive to this day whose properties can be explained with nanoscience.

  15. 500 BC

    In Ancient Greece, a philosopher called Leucippus and his pupil Democritus propose something literally groundbreaking: matter is made up of small indivisible elements called atoms, of which everything is composed and of which there are different kinds. If you cut an object down in size, again and again you would eventually arrive at these indestructible structures. It would take two and a half thousand years to prove how close they were.

  16. 1.8 million years ago...

    No-one knows how long ago our ancestor, homo erectus, first emerged. But it could have been almost 2 million years ago, and it could also have coincided with a remarkable thing which only humans can create and control – fire. At some stage we started to use fire to change the properties of the world around us.

    With fire we could cook our food and keep ourselves warm. We could see things that before were hidden. We might look a little different from our ancestors, but our compulsion to play, investigate and manipulate the world around us is very likely unchanged.
