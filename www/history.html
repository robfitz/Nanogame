A Brief History of Nano

  1. 2012

    Nanotech grows!

    There are a lot of Nanotech patents.

  2. 2007

    Nano-Power

    Angela Belcher and colleagues at MIT built a lithium-ion battery with a common type of virus that is nonharmful to humans, using a low-cost and environmentally benign process. The batteries have the same energy capacity and power performance as state-of-the-art rechargeable batteries being considered to power plug-in hybrid cars, and they could also be used to power personal electronic devices.

  3. 2004

    Nanotech Publications

    Britain’s Royal Society and the Royal Academy of Engineering publish Nanoscience and Nanotechnologies: Opportunities and Uncertainties advocating the need to address potential health, environmental, social, ethical, and regulatory issues associated with nanotechnology.

  4. 1999

    Consumer Nanotech

    Consumer products making use of nanotechnology began appearing in the marketplace, including lightweight nanotechnology-enabled automobile bumpers that resist denting and scratching, golf balls that fly straighter, tennis rackets that are stiffer (therefore, the ball rebounds faster), baseball bats with better flex and "kick," clear sunscreens, wrinkle- and stain-resistant clothing, scratch-resistant glass coatings, faster-recharging batteries for cordless electric tools, and digital cameras.

  5. 1986

    Atomic Force Microscope

    Atoms can now be manipulated directly.

  6. 1981

    Scanning/Tunneling Microscope

    Invented by Gerd Binning and Heinrich Rohrer at IBM’s research lab in Zurich. This invention allows scientists not only to observe nanoscale particles, atoms and small molecules, but to control them. The STM scans the tip of a needle or probe above the surface of a sample a voltage is applied between the tip and the surface as the current begins to flow, the STM can determine minute variations in t he distance electrons travel and thus maps the surface of the sample.

    Binning and Rohrer were awarded the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1986.

  7. 1974

    “Nanotecholoy”

    The term nanotechnology use for the first time by Norio Taniguchi of the Tokyo science university.

  8. 1951

    Field Ion Microscope.

    The development of nano technology has been dependent on advances in scientific instrumentation. In 1951 Erwin Muller invented the field ion-electron microscope. For the first time in history individual atoms and their arrangement on a surface could be seen. Magnification of more than 2,000,000 times.

  9. 1931

    Transmitting Electron Microscope

    Light microscopes are unable to recognise object that were smaller than the wavelength of visible light (0.4 – 0.7 micrometers) to see particles smaller than this scientists had to bypass light all together and use a different kind of “illumination” one with a shorter wavelength.

    In 1931 german scientists Max Knot and Erst Ruska developed and entirely new type of microscope that would eventually open up a new, small world. In an electron microscope electons are accelerated in a vacuum until their wavelength is extremely short. Beam of these fast moving electrons are focussed on a sample parts of a sample soak up the electrons other parts scatter them. An electron sensitive photographic plate records this action and creates and image

  10. 1904 - 1913

    Models of the Atom

    In 1904 J. J. Thomson proposes the ‘plum pudding’ model of the atom. In 1911 X Rutherford’s research proves plum pudding model to be incorrect and in 1913 Niels Bohr describes the atom as having electrons that travel in circular orbits around a small, positively charged nucleus.

  11. 1827

    Photography

    Joseph Niepce produces the first true photograph using film containing silver halides that light causes to decompose into nano particles of silver that form the pixels of the image.

  12. 1674

    First Microscope

    Anton van Leeuwenhoek invents microscope. Builds simple microscope with only one lens blood, yeast, insects…. First to see bacteria.

  13. 400 AD

    Lycurgus Cup

    The ancient Roman Lycurgus Cup changes from green when illuminated from the outside, to red, when illuminated from within. The glass contains nano particles of gold and silver, at the nano scale materials exhibit strange properties. Created by accident.

  14. 500 BC

    Atoms Imagined

    In Ancient Greece, Leucippus and his pupil Democritus propose that all matter is composed of small indivisible particles called atoms.

    INSTEAD OF ALL THE UNIVERSE BEING FULL OF ONE CONTINOUS STUFF. THERE ARE BITS WHERE THERE ARE SOME SMALL PARTICLES OF DIFFERENT KINDS WHICH CAN FLY AROUND OR BE STATIONARY AND THERE CAN BE GAPS

    SO IF YOU CUT CLAY AGAIN AND AGAIN AND AGAIN YOU'D EVENTUALLY GET TO AN INDIVISIBLE UNIT

    The theory of Democritus and Leucippus held that everything is composed of "atoms", which are physically, but not geometrically, indivisible; that between atoms lies empty space; that atoms are indestructible; have always been, and always will be, in motion; that there are an infinite number of atoms, and kinds of atoms, which differ in shape, and size

  15. 1.8 million years ago...

    Our ancestor, homo erectus emerged around this time. This could have coincided with the ability to make and use fire.

    These animals were somewhere between a climbing raw food chewing ape and an upright walking cooked food eating early human.

    With the use of fire came our ability not just to use the things around us, but to change them too. To create new materials and use them to manipulate and explore our world.
